<Article title="The fortified entrance" lang="en" ssrLang={props.cookies.lang}>
    The hillfort had two original entrances, one in the north and the other in the south; all other »openings« in the rampart are later in date. The main entrance was in the south. In front of it, ALS data revealed the access path, which is unique in its constructional features. It is a 4–5 m wide path identified in the length of some 180 m that begins at the iron-smelting area on Branževec and makes several turns before reaching the entrance. In this section, it is flanked by banks on both sides and additionally fortified with two transverse walls. The excavations of the one closer to the hillfort have unearthed a roughly 4 m wide stone wall. It is poorly preserved, but it is still possible to identify an inner and an outer face constructed of large stones, two lines of large stone along the middle and stone rubble filling the spaces in between, similarly as in the rampart.

    The embanked path offered additional protection for the inhabitants of Cvinger and also control over any visitors. It should be considered in association with other parts of the site, which was a carefully organised unit. ALS data also revealed holloways that converge into a corridor of movement and represent the remains of ancient paths. These lead past the barrow cemetery on Branževec, past the iron-smelting area and through the fortified entrance into the hillfort. Such a spatial organisation of a hillfort area is not unique, but offers an insight into an aspect of Iron Age society that has previously been poorly known. The visitor to Cvinger first had to pass through the necropolis (the world of the dead), which celebrated the glory of the community’s ancestors, continued past the smelteries as the source of the community’s prosperity and finally endured the »final test« along the embanked path before being allowed to enter the hillfort. This was a journey that the ancients specifically designed for their contemporaries, but one that may also intrigue the modern visitor.
