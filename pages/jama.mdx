Na naslednjo pisno obravnavo je jama čakala vse do leta 1991, ko je domačin Andrej Hudoklin v prispevku za Naše jame objavil kratek pregled do tedaj znanega dogajanja z obravnavano jamo. Ena izmed njegovih ugotovitev je bila, da je nasutje v jami nastalo po drugi svetovni vojni, kar so potrdili tudi ostanki eksplodiranih minskih teles. Ob tem je zabeležil tudi pripovedi, ki so o jami in njenih skrivnostih krožile med domačini.

    Cvingersko jamo so uradno prvi obiskali topliški jamarji, sicer člani Jamarskega kluba Novo mesto (1982). Za trikotnim vhodom (2 × 2,5 m) so izmerili 6 m globoko in 10 m dolgo vhodno brezno, v katerem pa zaradi očitnega nasutja z vrha ni bilo nadaljevanja. Ker je bila njena bližnja in širša okolica več stoletij obljudena, bi lahko v jamo po naključju (ali namerno) padli tudi razni predmeti iz različnih zgodovinskih obdobij. Zaradi svoje specifične lege je bil jami torej pripisan visok arheološki potencial. Med letoma 1986 in 1987 so se zato tudi jamarji vključili v arheološke raziskave, ki jih je takrat na Cvingerju vodil Borut Križ. V teh dveh poletnih sezonah so odkopavali nasutje, ki je domnevno prekrivalo arheološke plasti in ki je hkrati zapiralo prehod v nižje dele jame. Iz jame so s pomočjo vrvne tehnike izvlekli okoli 10 m3 materiala (zemlja, kamenje, komunalni odpadki), s čimer so jamo poglobili za več metrov. Ker so večje skale, ki jih niso mogli izvleči, začele groziti s podorom, so s kopanjem v jami prenehali. Prehod v morebitno jamsko nadaljevanje ali dostop do arheoloških plasti je zato tudi po zaključenih raziskavah ostal neznanka.

<Article title="The Cvinger Cave" lang="en" ssrLang={props.cookies.lang}>
    The hills of the Dolenjska region are dotted with prehistoric hillforts. As opposed to all others, the hillfort on Cvinger has a karst abyss that is located at its highest point, almost in the centre; it is called Cvinger (also Celtic) Cave. It is uncertain, however, whether it already existed in the Hallstatt period or was created later. It is a mystery that archaeologists and cavers from Novo mesto have been trying to solve during their recent joint investigations.

    <ArticleImage
        src={ograja}
        caption="Fence protecting the entrance to the Cvinger Cave. "
    />

    It is probably the location of the cave in the vicinity of a populated area that sparked the interest of some authors writing in the early 20th century. The earliest known written record is in the booklet titled Zdravišče Toplice (translated as The Toplice Spa), which Paul Zhuber von Okrog wrote in 1900. In the chapter on the surroundings of the spa, he stated that “the afore-mentioned Tabor hill gently rises just behind the park. Beautiful forest pathways lead amidst lush tall spruce trees from the road towards the top, where the former Celtic settlement is located. At the very top is a vertical cave reaching more than 20 metres deep. Because of the dangerous entrance, no one has explored it, though it is believed to be connected with horizontal cavities. The cave holds beautiful stalactites.” Less than a decade later (1909), Alfons Müllner published the book on the history of iron, already mentioned above, in which he provided a brief account of his visit (1873) to the prehistoric rampart and barrows accompanied by two drawings, one marking the geographic location of Cvinger and the other illustrating the rampart and a section across the settlement with the abyss visible at its centre. After almost eighty years, the next record dates to 1991 when a local, Andrej Hudoklin published a brief overview of what was known on the cave in his contribution in the Naše jame magazine. One of his observations was that the cave deposits were created after World War II, which was later confirmed by the remains of exploded shells. He also recorded the stories circulating among the locals on the cave and its secrets.

    <SketchFab lang="en"/>

    The first to officially visit the Cvinger Cave were the cavers from Toplice, who were members of the Novo mesto Caving Club (1982). After a triangular entrance (2 × 2.5 m), they measured a 6 m deep and 10 m long entrance abyss, which was closed off with the fill that had entered the abyss from above. Given its location in a populated area, objects from different historical periods could have fallen into the cave (or were thrown in intentionally); this made the cave an archaeological site of great potential. Archaeologists working at Cvinger between 1986 and 1987, under the leadership of Borut Križ, thus also involved cavers in their work. In two summer campaigns, they removed the fill believed to cover archaeological layers and block the passage to the lower-lying parts of the cave. Using the rope technique, roughly 10 m3 of material (earth, stones, communal waste) was removed, which deepened the cave for several metres.

    Work in the cave ceased for exactly three decades, until the cavers were again invited to join a team of archaeologists, now headed by Matija Črešnar (Department of Archaeology, Faculty of Arts, University of Ljubljana). The task was to continue clearing the fill from the entrance chamber. It was loaded into plastic barrels lifted from the cave using an electric winch and then transported via a horizontal cableway to a spot where archaeologists could examine the excavated material. In several campaigns in 2016 and 2017, new 10 m3 of material was removed and the cave deepened to the point of reaching the continuation of the cave in two spots. Speleological investigations ensued and revealed a continuation of the abyss between branching corrosion fissures and chambers. The largest interior space is the Boulder Hall, which is 15 m high. It terminates at the bottom in a mesh of passages that all end in unpassable constrictions. The newly discovered parts end in a shaft some 10 m under the collapsed blocks of the Boulder Hall, at a depth of 51 m.

    The cave failed to offer the results that the archaeologists had hoped for. The excavated material yielded numerous post-medieval objects and a single, large piece of a prehistoric vessel. There were no cultural layers in the newly-discovered parts of the cave, suggesting that they had previously been inaccessible. Experts believe that the entrance to the cave was either hidden at the time when people lived on Cvinger or that it only appeared when the cave ceiling collapsed, by which time the prehistoric community had long abandoned the settlement.
