    Cvingersko gomilno grobišče Branževec sodi med klasična starejšeželeznodobna najdišča Dolenjske, ki so s svojimi bogatimi najdbami v arheološkem svetu že ob koncu 19. stoletja sooblikovala pojem »cvetoči dolenjski halštat«. Do njega iz naselja navzdol vodi prazgodovinska pot, ki se mimo talilniškega območja proti jugu spusti do ledine Branževec nad današnjo vasjo Sela. Grobišče danes šteje 31 še vidnih zemljenih gomil. To so v tlorisu okrogle kopaste tvorbe s premerom 6 do 20 metrov in z ohranjeno višino do 4 metre. V času njihovega nastanka in uporabe so bile še precej višje. Z izkopavanji teh gomil je leta 1898 začel znani dolenjski starinokop Jernej Pečnik in v tem ter naslednjem letu izkopal 16 gomil ter še eno v Dolgih delih pri Meniški vasi. Med delom ga je nekajkrat obiskal že omenjeni Josef Szombathy, ki ga je nadziral in hkrati finančno podpiral. Izkopano gradivo je zato prišlo v zbirke Prirodoslovnega muzeja na Dunaju, kjer je del restavriranih najdb vključenih v stalno postavitev arheološke zbirke. Vsi predmeti iz gomil časovno sodijo v mlajše obdobje starejše železne dobe.

    Med izkopanimi predmeti prevladujejo keramične posode, ki so v veliki meri verjetno vsebovale hrano in pijačo kot popotnico umrlim za pot v onostranstvo. Praviloma so vse posode okrašene s plastičnim, vrezanim in slikanim okrasom. Pogosto imajo ročaje in stojijo na različno visokih nogah. Izdelovali so jih prostoročno v domačih lončarskih delavnicah, najdemo pa jih v moških in ženskih grobovih. Poleg keramičnih posod so bili v ženske grobove priloženi še nakitni predmeti: bronaste različno okrašene masivne in votle zapestnice in nanožnice, bronaste in železne fibule oz. zaponke različnih tipov in velikosti, bronasti lasni obročki ter bronasti okrašeni valjasti uhani in obeski. Ob njih najdemo še ogrlice, izdelane iz uvoženih jantarnih jagod ter iz raznobarvnih, bogato okrašenih in zelo verjetno na Dolenjskem izdelanih steklenih jagod. Med najznačilnejše grobne pridatke ženskih grobov sodijo tudi okrašena keramična vretenca (vztrajniki in uteži za preslico pri predenju). V moške grobove so bili poleg keramičnih posod priloženi še železno in bronasto orožje ter deli oblačil in opreme. Oborožitev bojevnika sta sestavljali železna tulasta ali plavutasta sekira ter ena ali dve sulici. K opremi je sodil ukrivljen železen nož in kamnit brus ter kovinska pasna garnitura – pasna plošča ali obročki in pasni jezički, ki so bili z zakovicami pritrjeni na pas.

    Posebno kategorijo pokojnikov predstavljajo pokopi, kjer se kot grobni pridatki pojavljajo bronaste čelade, konjska oprema ter bronasto posodje. V teh grobovih lahko prepoznamo vodilni sloj tedanje družbe – halštatske kneze. To so bili veljaki, ki so v svojih rokah združevali vojaško, politično in ekonomsko oblast. S prestižnimi pridatki so tudi v zagrobnem življenju predstavljali posebnost, ne le v okviru svoje družine, temveč v celotni skupnosti. Poleg izjemnih grobnih pridatkov sta status pokojnika nakazovali še njihova lega v gomili ter grobna arhitektura. Izpostaviti velja grob 9 v gomili V, ki je bil, kot je zapisal izkopavalec Pečnik, »prekrit kar s tremi vozovi kamenja«. Vseboval je čelado negovskega tipa, bronasto narebreno cisto, dve sulici, sekiro, nož, britev, bronaste fibule in pasno garnituro. Njemu je podoben grob 17 v isti gomili, le da gre v tem primeru za dvojni pokop (moškega in ženske). V njem se poleg značilnih moških grobnih pridatkov, kot so bronasta negovska čelada, dve železni sulici in sekira, dva železna ražnja, nož in britev, deli pasu in obročki ter bronasta situla in bronasta kotlička, pojavljajo še značilni ženski pridatki, kot so jantarne ogrlice ter votle in masivne bronaste zapestnice.

    Posebnost predstavlja tudi grob 23 v gomili II. V tem, po pridatkih sodeč moškem grobu, sta ležali dve železni sulični osti, železen nož, dve bronasti certoški fibuli, bronasti pasni jezički, železna ostroga ter bronasta figuralno okrašena situla. Ta je skupaj s situlama iz Vač in z Magdalenske gore ena najlepših v Sloveniji. Situlski spomenik ima plašč, ki je okrašen s tremi figuralnimi frizi. Na zgornjem je prikazan sprevod jezdecev in enoosnih vozov s konjsko vprego. Prvi v sprevodu, ki se premika v levo, je pešec, sledi mu vprežni dvokolesni voz s potnikom ter na voz stopajočo osebo z dolgo palico. Sledita konja, ki ju na povodcu vodita moža, ter slabo viden vprežni voz, ki mu sledi še konjenik. Vse osebe so moškega spola in pokrite z baretkam podobnimi pokrivali. V drugem, osrednjem frizu, ki je precej poškodovan, je predstavljen sprevod pešcev ter jezdec. V spodnjem, poškodovanem frizu je znana scena lovca z lokom in šolanim lovskim psom ter trop rogatih živali. Situla je okrašena v klasičnem situlskem stilu s prehoda iz 6. v 5. stoletje pr. n. št. Med situlske spomenike iz grobov na Cvingerju sodi še figuralno okrašena pasna spona iz groba 21 gomile XI, ki pa je slabše izdelana, saj so konji na njej dokaj slabo prikazani. Na tem grobišču so poleg opisanih našli še tri bronaste situle z neokrašenimi plašči, dva bronasta kotlička, tri bronaste čelade, bogato konjsko opremo, množico orožja, nakitnih predmetov ter keramičnega posodja.

<Article title="The barrow cemetery" lang="en" ssrLang={props.cookies.lang}>
    The barrow cemetery at Branževec ranks among the classic Early Iron Age sites with an abundance of finds that have shaped the phenomenon of the ‘flourishing Hallstatt of Dolenjska’ ever since the end of the 19th century. The cemetery is accessed from the settlement via a prehistoric path that descends southwards past the smelting area to reach the Branževec site located above the modern-day village of Sela. The cemetery is composed of 31 still visible barrows. These are earthen mounds of a round plan measuring 6 to 20 metres across and up to 4 metres high. At the time of their creation and use, they would have been considerably higher. The first to excavate them was Jernej Pečnik, a self-taught archaeologist from Dolenjska. In 1898 and 1899, he excavated sixteen barrows and one also at Dolgi deli near Meniška vas. During his work, he received several visits by Josef Szombathy, curator at the Natural History Museum in Vienna, who supervised his work and provided financial support. The excavated goods therefore went to the Viennese museum, where some of the restored objects today form part of the permanent archaeological collection. All artefacts from the barrows on Branževec date to the late period of the Early Iron Age. 

    <ArticleImage
        src={lidar_EN}
        caption="Spatial distribution of the barrows shown on a digital terrain model."
    />

    Predominating among the excavated grave goods is pottery, most of which is believed to have contained food and drink as provisions for the afterlife. Almost all vessels bear raised, incised or painted decoration, often also handles and differently high pedestals. They were made by hand in the local pottery workshops and offered in the graves of both men and women. In addition to pottery, the graves of women also held pieces of jewellery such as differently shaped bronze bracelets and armlets that were either solid or hollow, as well as bronze and iron brooches of different types and sizes, bronze hair rings, pendants and decorated bronze cylindrical earrings. Also found in female burials were necklaces made of imported amber bead, and multi-coloured richly decorated glass beads that were most likely made in Dolenjska. The typical goods in the graves of women include decorated ceramic spindle whorls (implements that increased and maintained the speed of the spindle). In the graves of men, pottery was accompanied by iron and bronze weapons, as well as pieces of costume and equipment. The outfit of a warrior consisted of an iron socketed or winged axe and one or two spears, in some cases also a curved iron knife and a whetstone, as well as a belt, of which only the belt plate or rings and strap ends survive, while the leather strap disintegrated.

        src={gomila}
        caption="One of the most clearly distinguishable barrows of the Branževec cemetery."
    />

    A special category of the population was that buried with bronze helmets, horse equipment and bronze vessels. These individuals represent the upper echelons of contemporary society – the Hallstatt princes. They were dignitaries who held military, political and economic authority. With the grave goods of prestige, they continued to stand out in their family and the community as a whole even in the afterlife. In addition to grave goods, their special status was underscored by the location of the burial within the barrow, as well as the form of burial. An example of this is Grave 9 in Barrow V, which Pečnik reports to have been ‘covered with as many as three cartfuls of stones’. The individual was buried with a helmet of the Negova type, a ribbed bronze cist, two spears, an axe, a knife, a razor, bronze brooches and a belt. Similar to it is Grave 17 in the same barrow, which held a double burial, of a man and a woman. This grave contained characteristic male goods, such as a bronze Negova helmet, two iron spears and an axe, two iron spits, a knife and a razor, parts of the belt, as well as rings, a bronze situla and two small bronze cauldrons, but also typical female goods, such as amber beads, as well as hollow and solid bronze bracelets.

    Another burial of particular importance was that in Grave 23, Barrow II. The grave goods, namely two iron spearheads, an iron knife, two bronze Certosa brooches, bronze strap ends, an iron spur and a bronze situla with figural decoration, suggest it was the last resting place of a man. Alongside the two examples from Vače and Magdalenska gora, it is among the most beautiful situlae in Slovenia. The body of the situla is divided into three figural friezes. The top one shows a cortège of horsemen and horse-drawn chariots. The first man in the procession, moving to the left, is on foot and followed by a chariot carrying a passenger and one other individual with a long cane on the verge of mounting the chariot. This is followed by two horses with two men leading them by the reins, a poorly visible wagon and finally another mounted horseman. All human figures are men and all wear beret-like headgear. The central frieze, which is heavily damaged, shows a procession of footmen and a mounted horseman. Also heavily damaged, the lower frieze presents a well-known scene of an archer, a trained hunting dog and a herd of horned animals. The situla is decorated in the classic situla style dating to the transition from the 6th to the 5th century BC. Another monument of situla art has come to light at Cvinger – a belt plate with figural decoration from Grave 21 in Barrow XI; the very schematically rendered horses on the plate suggest slightly poorer workmanship. The cemetery revealed three more situlae, but without decoration, two bronze cauldrons, three bronze helmets, rich horse gear, as well as a multitude of weapons, jewellery and pottery.

    A special find in the Cvinger area is a ceramic vessel that was dug into a prehistoric barrow and differs substantially in form, production manner and decoration from the pottery recovered from the prehistoric graves. It appears that this vessel, typical of the 7th century AD, was dug into a barrow long after the barrow had fallen into disuse and as such represents one of the first known cremation burials of the Slavs in Slovenia.
