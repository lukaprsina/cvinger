<Article title="The smelting area" lang="en" ssrLang={props.cookies.lang}>
    Iron smelting and blacksmithing were among the most important activities of Cvinger’s prehistoric inhabitants. Traces of these activities have come to light both inside the settlement, where slag can be found on the surface, but even more on the southern slope of the hill. Roughly halfway between the settlement and the barrow cemetery at Branževec, archaeological field surveys in 1988 recovered large quantities of iron slag on the surface. The slag was mixed with pieces of burnt loam, which suggested the remains of Iron Age iron-smelting furnaces under the forest humus.

    To verify this hypothesis, a trial trench (3 × 4 m) was excavated at the centre of the maximum concentration of surface finds. It revealed great quantities of slag and burnt loam just under the turf, which were scattered across the trench. The outlines of twelve iron smelting furnaces appeared at the level of orange-red loam (50‒60 cm below the surface). They stood close to one another, two were even touching. Further excavation showed that only the lower parts of the furnaces survived, which were pits dug into loamy soil with clay lining the walls; the walls were for the most part heavily burnt and the pits filled with darker soil, pieces of slag, wood charcoal and at places also burnt stones. In plan, most furnaces were round and measured 40 to 70 cm in diameter, one only was larger, with 80 × 100 cm.

    <ArticleImage
        src={vzorci}
        caption="Display of iron in its different forms and weights."

    The archaeological trial trenching confirmed the existence of a smelting complex on Branževec. The subsequent non-destructive geophysical investigations (using the magnetic method, 1997) showed that the complex extended across roughly 0.6 ha and comprised several hundreds of smelting furnaces. Iron extraction thus took place outside the settlement, while the obtained raw iron (bloom) was processed and forged also inside the hillfort.

    <ArticleImage
        src={talilnik}
        caption="Section through the smelting furnace with the slag pit, at the beginning and end of the smelting process."
    />

    Further archaeological investigations took place on Branževec from 2013 to 2018 and involved the most advanced geophysical methods. The results were used as the basis for new excavations, which again showed densely spaced smelting surfaces. They also showed that some furnaces were used several times and even when a furnace was no longer functional, it was in some cases partially reused or a new one constructed next to it.

    <ArticleImage
        src={raziskave}
        caption="Archaeological trial trenching in the smelting area. Photo by Matija Črešnar."
    />

    One of the furnaces was also dated using the magnetic dating method (for the first time in Slovenia). The result corresponds with the archaeological evidence and suggests the furnace was last used in 640–370 BC. The success of this dating attempt was all the more significant in view of the absence of chronologically diagnostic ceramic or metal finds in the smelting area and the fact that the radiocarbon dating of charcoal is limited for the period in question.
